When does the baby's heartbeat start?

The most important concern of expectant mothers in the first trimester is the heartbeat of the baby. Back in the day stethoscope was the only device to detect the baby's heartbeat and only doctors could hear the baby's heartbeat with it. Now with the advancement of science and the invention of various devices it is possible to detect the heartbeat of the baby much earlier and even mothers can hear the heartbeat of the baby in the womb. When does the baby's heartbeat start?

The baby's heart begins to form in the fifth week of pregnancy. The baby's heartbeat usually starts within 22-24 days of conception i.e. by the sixth week of pregnancy. But at this time, the baby's heart is so small that it cannot produce sounds that can be detected by an ultrasound or Doppler machine.

If an ultrasound is done before 8 weeks of pregnancy, the baby's heartbeat can be felt at this time. A Doppler machine can detect a baby's heartbeat by 10 weeks, but usually by 12 weeks. How soon the baby's heartbeat is picked up by the machine depends on the position of the baby in the mother's uterus, the position of the placenta and the mother's weight.

If you can't hear your baby's heartbeat at 8 or 10 weeks, there's no need to panic. The baby may be in a position in your uterus with its back facing your stomach This makes it difficult to easily detect the heartbeat on a doppler machine. After a few weeks, you may hear that desired sound. In such cases, the doctor will check everything and see if everything is fine.

  • Heart Rate Chart of Pregnant Baby:

    First two semesters, the heartbeat rate is around 103-126bpm. In the third trimester, the heartbeat is more stable. However, the heartbeat decreases slightly before delivery. In full term pregnancy the baby's heart rate at delivery is around 140 bpm and in pre-term pregnancy it can be around 155 bpm. How well the baby's heartbeat is perceived depends on the position of the baby, the position of the placenta and the mother's physical condition. Generally, if the baby's heart rate is between 100-160, it is considered normal.

  • Congenital heart disease:

    The baby's heartbeat is usually checked during an ultrasound at 6 to 9 weeks of pregnancy. At this time, the mother's due date is also determined. During the second trimester or 20 weeks ultrasound, the doctor will check the structure of the baby's heart in the womb to see if there are any defects in the baby's heart. At this time, there is no way to fix any defects during pregnancy, and some defects are not even detected before the baby is born, but if something is detected, it is easier for doctors to determine what kind of treatment the baby will need right. after birth.

  • How to keep the baby's heart healthy during pregnancy?:

    Some factors may be out of your control, such as chromosomal and genetic abnormalities. However, you can try to keep your unborn baby's heart healthy by following some rules- Getting enough folic acid is very important if you are pregnant or planning to become pregnant. Folic acid during pregnancy helps reduce the risk of several heart-related complications in addition to neural tube defects (NTD).

    • If any parent has a smoking habit, quit it immediately. Studies have shown that 2 percent of congenital heart disease is caused by smoking of father or mother.
    • If there is diabetes during pregnancy, sugar level should be kept under control. Gestational diabetes greatly increases the risk of congenital heart disease.
    • Avoid alcohol or drugs, try to eat nutritious and balanced food Some studies have shown that mothers who exercise regularly during pregnancy have a higher rate of heart health in their children. But remember to consult an expert before exercising.



Can you tell if the baby is a boy or a girl by looking at the baby's heartbeat? Many say that if the baby's heart rate is greater than or equal to 140 BPM, then the baby is a girl. And if the heart rate is less than 140 BPM, then the fetus will be a boy. This is a misconception because the baby's gender has no effect on heart rate in the first trimester. All babies have a high heart rate from 28-30 weeks. Up to the fifth week of pregnancy, the heart rate of the fetus is very close to that of the mother at 80-85 BPM. By the ninth week it continues to increase and may reach up to 170-200 BPM. It then decreases to 120-160 BPM in mid-pregnancy. A 1999 study found that just before delivery, the heart rate of female babies is higher than that of male babies.
